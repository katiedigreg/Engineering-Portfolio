Design Experiences

This section documents my primary engineering design activities. For each project, I have detailed the context, summarized the outcomes, and evaluated the specific Concepts, Tools, Models, and Frameworks (CTMFs) that guided my team's process[cite: 7, 72, 76].

Praxis I: Hand-Held Broom Optimization

Context in my "Volleyball Season": This was my first exposure to open-ended engineering design. It challenged my assumption that problems are clearly defined and forced me to confront my bias of jumping straight to execution without proper planning or divergence[cite: 1142, 1148].

Background & Opportunity

Hand-held brooms (HHBs) are notoriously unstable when rested upright against a wall due to their small base and uneven bristles[cite: 291, 292]. When they fall, retrieving them requires five steps and an uncomfortable bending motion[cite: 294]. This presents a significant challenge for our primary stakeholders: Engineering Science students experiencing mild to moderate leg and lower back pain, for whom bending causes unnecessary stress and discomfort[cite: 544, 548, 563]. The opportunity was to design a mechanism to prevent HHBs from falling[cite: 1129].

Rigorous Framing

To ensure our design truly solved the problem, we established strict Requirements and Evaluation Criteria (ECs)[cite: 1130]. The attachment needed to:

  • Withstand an accidental sideways force of at least 1.5 N without tipping[cite: 313].
  • Allow the broom to be retrieved in under 5 steps to ensure convenience[cite: 315].
  • Maintain compactability (base < 0.35m x 0.35m)[cite: 321].
  • Add minimal weight, keeping the total broom weight under 2.3 kg to prevent ergonomic strain[cite: 323, 669].

Diverging & Exploring Possibilities

Guided by our framing, we engaged in structured divergence to generate multiple potential solutions[cite: 1135]. We developed four distinct concepts[cite: 1134]:

1. Suction Cup

A foot-actuated nitrile suction cup that creates a firm seal with the floor[cite: 337, 338].

2. Tripod Stand

A collapsible, three-legged aluminum system that deploys outward[cite: 367, 369].

3. Weighted Base

A heavy, High-Density Polyethylene block attached to the broom handle[cite: 383, 385].

4. Magnetic Stand

A standalone pole utilizing Neodymium magnets to hold the broom upright[cite: 395, 397].

Converging on a Solution

We utilized a Measurement Matrix, Pugh Charts, and Pairwise Comparison to evaluate our designs against our ECs[cite: 435, 441, 451, 1138]. While the Magnetic Stand was the fastest to retrieve (2 steps) [cite: 433, 457], horizontal force resistance (EC1.2) was our most critical metric[cite: 414, 415]. Engineering calculations revealed the Suction Cup could withstand an incredible 14.33 N of horizontal force—vastly outperforming the others (which all withstood under 2.1 N) while remaining exceptionally lightweight at 0.471 kg[cite: 423, 425, 427, 437, 438]. Consequently, the Suction Cup was selected as the optimal recommendation[cite: 458, 464].

Influence of My Position & What I Learned

I entered this project heavily biased. Once I visualized the suction cup idea, I didn't want to challenge it, and I actively resisted the planning phase in favor of jumping straight into building[cite: 1141, 1142]. My groupmates had to pull me back and enforce the divergence process[cite: 1144].

Through this, I had a major realization: while the suction cup *did* ultimately win, structured divergence revealed that other designs (like the magnetic stand) actually met specific requirements better[cite: 1145]. I learned the critical importance of extensive planning and evaluating options through rigorous metrics. You cannot just jump to a final design based on intuition; you must justify it[cite: 1148]. This perfectly encapsulates the "Practice" and "Timeout" phases of my design philosophy.

CTMFs Assessed

  • 1. Measurement Matrix Context: We used a Measurement Matrix to quantify the physical capabilities of our four designs (e.g., calculating exactly how many Newtons of force each could withstand and their total weights)[cite: 435].

    Assessment: This tool was incredibly effective. As someone who previously relied on intuition, seeing the raw math—that the suction cup held 14.33 N compared to the tripod's 1.717 N—provided undeniable, objective evidence[cite: 437]. It removed personal bias and grounded our convergence in reality. I will continue to use this tool to validate structural assumptions.
  • 2. Pugh Charts Context: We utilized a series of Pugh Charts, rotating the reference design, to compare our concepts against our qualitative and quantitative Evaluation Criteria[cite: 441, 442, 809].

    Assessment: Pugh charts were highly valuable for visualizing trade-offs. It clearly highlighted the suction cup's weakness regarding compatibility with different brooms (EC2.1)[cite: 445, 446], while showcasing its strength in stability. It forced me to acknowledge the flaws in my favored design, which is essential for the "Reflection" phase of my design process.
  • 3. Pairwise Comparison Context: After analyzing the Pugh charts and Measurement Matrix, we used Pairwise Comparison for our final convergence to rank the holistic viability of the four designs[cite: 451, 452].

    Assessment: Because we had already established that horizontal force resistance was our most critical metric [cite: 414], the Pairwise Comparison provided a clean, definitive framework to eliminate the weaker designs (like the tripod and magnetic stand) [cite: 456, 457] and confidently select the suction cup[cite: 458]. It acts as an excellent final "Timeout" before committing to a design direction.
