Position Statement

"When my team needs a moment to ground ourselves during a match, my coach calls a timeout. During the timeout, motion pauses. We breathe. We refocus. Intention replaces panic. I approach engineering design through this same lens."

Introduction

Hello, my name is Katie. I am incredibly grateful for the privilege of playing on the UofT women's varsity volleyball team. Competitive athletics has placed me in high-pressure situations where resilience, focus, and trust are constantly tested.

Problems are not solved by rushing towards solutions. Meaningful design requires us to pause, reflect, reframe, and then act. Just as timeouts are essential to regaining control in a game, reflection is essential in navigating engineering design. Design is not linear; it is an iterative process, requiring moments of stepping back in order to move forward with purpose.

The Timeout Philosophy

Over my years of playing volleyball, my coaches have given me advice that translates directly into my engineering practice:

1. Do not fear failure. Stick to our system.

In the game of volleyball, mistakes are inevitable. We miss serves, shank passes, and errors happen publicly. In these moments, my coach reminds us not to let the past point define the next one. I bring this mindset directly into engineering design. Because design is inherently iterative, a failed design is not a sign of incompetence, but an opportunity to learn. I value disciplined practice: testing assumptions, refining approaches, and committing to continuous improvement.

2. Be a great teammate. Constant Communication.

Being a part of my volleyball team requires constant communication and mutual trust on and off the court. Leadership is doing my part while supporting others in doing theirs. As engineering design requires strong teamwork and collaboration, these lessons shape how I work in design teams. I pay attention to group dynamics, address problems early, and create space for groupmates to openly contribute. Trust is not a byproduct of teamwork; it is a condition for it.

3. Control the controllables.

During a game, I cannot control the referees or the opponents. However, I can control being ready in the right position on defence. The same is true in engineering design. I cannot control stakeholder needs, unexpected constraints, or unforeseen failures. But I can control how I frame opportunities, test assumptions, and respond to feedback. When design work feels uncertain, I interpret that discomfort as a signal that the opportunity has not yet been fully understood—an invitation to pause, reflect, and reframe.

4. Believe. Be brave.

Volleyball led me to discover my faith in God, particularly in moments of pressure where my team depended on me to perform. This perspective has reshaped how I engage in engineering design. Design is a long and uncertain process, and an excessive focus on outcomes can obscure learning along the way. My faith reminds me to value the journey, remain grateful for the opportunity to learn and create, and to persist without fear.
